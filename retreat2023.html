Interactive Speed Querying on High-Dimensional Data with Partially Materialized Data Cubes

In this talk, Sachin Basil John will present Sudokube, a novel data cube system for interactive querying of high-dimensional data. Sudokube chooses which projections to precompute and materialize based on a given storage budget, and then uses them to approximate query results at runtime, reducing both compute and storage requirements compared to fully materialized data cubes. Sudokube supports a large number of dimensions, allowing users to load denormalized data directly without the need to carefully select a minimal number of dimensions. Support for denormalized data also results in faster queries as they avoid expensive joins. This makes Sudokube a valuable tool for OLAP and other scenarios such as exploring high-dimensional data for feature engineering.

Generalizing Bulk-Synchronous Parallel Processing for Data Science: From Data to Threads and Agent-Based Simulations

In this talk, Tian Zilu will present a generalization of the bulk-synchronous parallel (BSP) processing model to better support agent-based simulations. Such simulations frequently exhibit hierarchical structure in their communication patterns which can be exploited to improve performance. They allow for the creation of temporary artificial network partitions during which agents synchronize only locally within their group in a way that does not compromise the correctness of a simulation. They have built a distributed engine, CloudCity, which uses this idea to improve the locality of computation, communication, and synchronization in such simulations. They experimentally evaluate the performance of their system on a benchmark of simulation workloads and compare it against other popular BSP-like systems, obtaining insights into the impact of various system design choices and optimization on simulation engine performance.

Reinforcement Learning for Factorized Databases

In this talk, Christoph Mayer will introduce an approach based on reinforcement learning for finding the optimal variable order for join queries in factorized databases. This problem is NP-hard and remains a major challenge in database research. Their approach tries out several variable orders while executing the query and eventually converges to a near-optimal variable order.

Quasi-stable Coloring for Graph Compression

In this talk, Moe Kayali will propose quasi-stable coloring, an approximate version of stable coloring, a well-studied technique in graph theory for classifying vertices, which can be used to build compact, lossless representations of graphs. However, its usefulness is limited due to its reliance on strict symmetries. Real data compresses very poorly using color refinement. They propose the first, to their knowledge, approximate color refinement scheme, which they call quasi-stable coloring. By using approximation, they alleviate the need for strict symmetry, and allow for a tradeoff between the degree of compression and the accuracy of the representation. They study three applications: Linear Programming, Max-Flow, and Betweenness Centrality, and provide theoretical evidence in each case that a quasi-stable coloring can lead to good approximations on the reduced graph. Next, they consider how to compute a maximal quasi-stable coloring: they prove that, in general, this problem is NP-hard, and propose a simple, yet effective algorithm based on heuristics. Finally, they evaluate experimentally the quasi-stable coloring technique on several real graphs and applications, comparing with prior approximation techniques.

Teaching reductions: Formal foundations

In this talk, Nils Vortmeier explores formal foundations for offering support in teaching students how to design computational reductions. Their approach is to (a) identify typical building blocks of reductions, (b) develop a simple descriptional language for specifying reductions that allows for combining building blocks in a simple, modular manner, (c) study the expressive power of such a language to ensure that it is reasonably broad, and to (d) design suitable algorithms for employing this language in teaching support systems, including the design of feedback mechanisms.

Probabilistic Query Evaluation with Bag Semantics

In this talk, Peter Lindner studies query evaluation on probabilistic databases with duplicates. In this framework, the answer to a Boolean query is a non-negative, integer-valued random variable. Hence, the problem comes in two flavors: computing expected answer counts, and computing answer count probabilities. Subject to mild assumptions, he finds that computing expected answer counts is possible in polynomial time. For computing answer count probabilities, he can show that the little dichotomy theorem of Dalvi and Suciu for self-join free conjunctive queries still holds in the bag semantics framework.

Unifying Data Discovery and Exploration with Foundation Models

In this talk, Moe Kayali explores the application of foundation models to data discovery and exploration tasks. Foundation models are large language models (LLMs) that show promising performance on a range of diverse tasks unrelated to their training. He shows that these models are highly applicable to the data discovery and data exploration domain. When carefully used, they have superior capability on three representative tasks: table-class detection, column-type annotation and join-column prediction. On all three tasks, he shows that a foundation-model-based approach outperforms the task-specific models and the state-of-the-art. Further, his approach often surpasses human-expert task performance. This suggests a future direction in which disparate data management tasks can be unified under foundation models.

Challenges of putting IVM into production

In this talk, Niko Göbel provides an overview of the operational challenges of incremental view maintenance (IVM) that RelationalAI has encountered. Even with incrementality built-in from the ground up, many unexpected challenges remain before things like IVM actually deliver user value. To customers, the operational benefits of traditional batch processing put the bar for IVM adoption much higher than simply "it runs faster". Niko raises some open questions that he thinks are underrepresented in research on this topic.

Ranking Database Facts by their Shapley Values

In this talk, Ahmet Kara will present a novel approach to measure the contribution of database facts to query results using the concept of Shapley value. Shapley value is a widely used concept in economics and machine learning to measure the contribution of each player in a cooperative game to the overall gain. The talk will address the following problems:

  1. What is the computational complexity of ranking database facts based on their Shapley values?
  2. What is the computational difference between the Shapley value and the Banzhaf power index?
  3. Can we design an efficient ranking algorithm that can scale to large real-world datasets?
The talk will also present some preliminary results and discuss potential future directions for research.

ExpressQuery: Query Answering with Knowledge Graphs

As availability of structured data grows, it is becoming increasingly important to query it for applications such as analytics and knowledge discovery. At the same time, the schemas for the underlying databases and Knowledge Graphs (KG) are often complicated. This is why in recent years, there's a significant interest towards Query Answering (QA) on large KGs via natural language interfaces, such as NLP to SQL converters.

In this work, we combine the power of LLMs for commonsense basic reasoning, and the interpretability and structure of database systems, and propose ExpressQuery, a system that allows formulating structured queries with very loose natural language constraints, allowing the query writers to express their intent without learning potentially very complicated database schema.

Along with the system, we propose a corresponding query language, ExpressQL. We validate our system on a snapshot of Wikidata Knowledge Graph (KG), one of the largest publicly available general-purpose knowledge graphs.
